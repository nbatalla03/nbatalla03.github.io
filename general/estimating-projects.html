Estimating Projects

I've sat in on a few IPMs here at 8th Light. One of the things that has really intrigued me is coming up with project estimates. A project estimate a lot like a forecast, you're making an educated guess to an outcome in the future with data you have in the present.

The goal with estimates is to be as accurate as possible, especially when client resources are on the line. So what can you do to increase your chances of a good estimate?

Have Three Different Mindsets

Estimations are based on points over three different scenarios - optimistic, realistic, pessimistic. Ryan, a craftsman at 8th Light gave this awesome tip of thinking in three different mindsets. By deliberately and honestly walking through each scenario, you can increase the accuracy of your estimate.

Lets take an optimistic approach for an example. Let's say your iteration requires you to clean up the styling on a page. An optimistic mindset may think, "The codebase is probably well written and I'll be able to figure things out relatively quickly with a simple glance." On the flip side, a pessmistic mindset may think, "I'm willing to bet that the code is absolutely terrible, I'm pretty sure I should just scrap everything and start over."

I know the above examples are a bit extreme, but I really wanted to illustrate getting out of a mindset and into another.

Two Heads Are Better

We pair programming pretty regularly here at 8th Light, this pairing segways into IPMs. Having an extra head opens up diaglogue to break down the problem from different angles. It's important that the two of you provide constant feedback and keep the flow going.

Talk It Through

This final tip piggybacks off the second. If you are making estimations on your own, a good way to go about your estimation is to throughly talk through your thought process in front of the client. Doing this helps flesh out your thoughts as well as bring confidence to the client by hand holding them through the process.
